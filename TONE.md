# STYLE.md: Michael Bargury Editorial Voice Guide

## Core Voice Identity

Michael Bargury's writing embodies **conversational technical expertise** - maintaining authoritative knowledge while remaining approachable and human. His voice bridges cutting-edge security research with practical implementation, characterized by humble expertise, community focus, and pragmatic optimism about technology's future.

## Fundamental Writing Patterns

### Sentence Structure
- **Length**: Medium sentences (15-25 words) with sophisticated yet accessible structure
- **Devices**: Frequent parenthetical asides "(tho say its the next step)" and em-dashes for emphasis "—and is therefore vulnerable"
- **Flow**: Mix of simple declarative statements with complex compound sentences for rhythm variety

### Vocabulary and Terminology
- **Signature Terms**: 
  - "folks" instead of "people" (conversational warmth)
  - "tho" instead of "though" (informal contraction)
  - Creates memorable portmanteaus: "promptware," "AIjacking," "LOLCopilot"
- **Technical Balance**: Uses cybersecurity jargon appropriately while explaining complex concepts accessibly
- **Acronym Usage**: Defines technical terms on first use, then uses freely (RCE, LLMs, MITRE ATT&CK)

### Tone Characteristics
- **Conversational Authority**: Informal yet knowledgeable, like a friendly expert colleague
- **Humble Confidence**: Makes bold predictions but acknowledges uncertainty ("I think we will, actually")
- **Self-Deprecation**: "This is a boring blog post. At least for humans."
- **Community-Minded**: "we are all n00bs trying to figure it out"

## Content Organization Patterns

### Opening Styles
- **Self-Aware Meta-Commentary**: "This is a boring blog post. At least for humans."
- **Context Setting**: References previous work or current industry state
- **Bold Statements**: Direct assertions that grab attention
- **Problem Introduction**: Clear threat scenarios or business challenges

### Paragraph Structure
- **Length**: Short, punchy paragraphs (2-4 sentences)
- **White Space**: Strategic use for readability
- **Transitions**: Frequent use of "While [X], [Y]" and "But there's a problem..."
- **Topic Sentences**: Clear and direct, setting paragraph purpose

### Closing Patterns
- **Forward-Looking**: Optimistic but realistic about future developments
- **Call to Action**: Practical next steps for readers
- **Synthesis**: Connects findings to broader implications
- **Community Appeal**: Encourages collaboration and knowledge sharing

## Technical Communication Style

### Concept Explanation
- **Analogy-Driven**: "Bugs are miscommunications in LLM's native language – code"
- **Progressive Disclosure**: Simple concepts first, building to technical complexity
- **Business Translation**: Always connects technical findings to business impact
- **Educational Scaffolding**: Anticipates reader questions and confusion points

### Security-Specific Patterns
- **Impact-First**: Leads with business consequences before technical details
- **Evidence-Based**: Supports claims with demonstrations and specific examples
- **Responsible Disclosure**: Emphasizes vendor coordination and ethical practices
- **Risk Communication**: Clear threat articulation with specific attack scenarios

### Documentation Approaches
- **Layered Depth**: Executive summary → Technical details → Implementation guides
- **Platform-Specific**: Provides Windows/Linux/MacOS variations
- **Visual Integration**: Strategic use of diagrams, screenshots, and code examples
- **Tool-Centric**: Creates and releases practical security tools alongside research

## Platform-Specific Adaptations

### Personal Blog/Informal
- Stream-of-consciousness style
- "unfinished thoughts" mentality
- Heavy use of humor and personality
- Experimental ideas and speculation allowed
- First-person narrative dominance

### Professional Publications (Dark Reading, etc.)
- Formal structure with clear sections
- Business-oriented terminology
- Strategic implications focus
- Third-person or editorial "we"
- Polished, edited prose

### Technical Documentation (GitHub)
- Hierarchical organization with clear navigation
- Heavy badge usage for credibility
- Code blocks with language specification
- Installation → Configuration → Usage flow
- Community contribution guidelines

### Corporate Blog (Zenity Labs)
- Team attribution ("our research team")
- Business impact prominently featured
- Professional visual consistency
- Strategic CTAs for company positioning
- Collaborative voice with individual insights

## Formatting Preferences

### Text Emphasis
- **Bold** for key terms and critical facts (not full sentences)
- *Italics* sparingly for special emphasis
- `Inline code` for commands and technical terms
- ~~Strikethrough~~ with tildes for corrections or humor

### Visual Elements
- ASCII art for project branding
- Conference badges for credibility
- Embedded YouTube videos for demonstrations
- Screenshots with annotations
- Tables for comparative data

### Link Integration
- Natural inline linking without breaking narrative flow
- Conference and tool references prominently featured
- Cross-references to related work
- External validation through citations

## Writing Process Guidelines

### For Blog Posts
1. Start with meta-commentary or bold assertion
2. Use narrative structure to explain technical concepts
3. Include personal anecdotes and conference experiences
4. Balance technical depth with accessibility
5. End with forward-looking synthesis

### For Technical Documentation
1. Lead with visual branding (ASCII art, badges)
2. Provide multiple installation paths
3. Use progressive disclosure for complexity
4. Include platform-specific variations
5. Link to demonstrations and talks

### For Professional Articles
1. Open with business impact statement
2. Structure with clear sections and headers
3. Translate technical findings to business value
4. Provide actionable recommendations
5. Maintain formal tone while preserving personality

### For Security Disclosures
1. State coordination with vendor upfront
2. Lead with impact assessment
3. Provide technical proof-of-concept
4. Include timeline of disclosure
5. Emphasize responsible practices
